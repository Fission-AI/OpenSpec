`/opsx:explore` is your thinking partner. Reach for it whenever you have a problem but not yet a plan. It investigates your codebase, weighs options with you, and clarifies what you actually want — all before a single artifact or line of code is created. When the picture is clear, it hands off to `/opsx:propose`.

<Callout title="If you take one habit from these docs, take this one">
When you are not sure, **explore before you propose.** AI assistants are eager — ask vaguely and they will confidently build *something*, just maybe not the thing you needed. Explore is the cure.
</Callout>

## When to explore

Explore is the right first step more often than people expect. Use it when any of these is true:

- You know the *problem* but not the *solution*. ("Pages feel slow." "Auth is a mess." "We keep getting duplicate orders.")
- You are choosing between approaches and want the tradeoffs laid out against your actual code.
- You are new to a codebase and need to understand how something works before you change it.
- The requirements are fuzzy and you want to sharpen them before committing.
- You suspect the work is bigger or smaller than it looks and want to scope it honestly.

Skip explore only when you already know exactly what you want and how. Then go straight to `/opsx:propose`.

## What it does (and does not)

Explore is a **conversation**, not a generator.

<Cards>
  <Card title="It does" icon="Check">
    Read and search your codebase, compare options and name tradeoffs, draw diagrams, narrow a vague idea into a buildable scope, and hand off to `/opsx:propose` when you are ready.
  </Card>
  <Card title="It does not" icon="X">
    Create a change folder, write any artifacts, or touch your code. Exploring costs nothing and commits you to nothing.
  </Card>
</Cards>

That is the point. You can explore three dead ends, learn something from each, and only then propose the path that survived.

It ships in the default **core** profile, so there is nothing to enable. If OpenSpec is set up in your project, explore is ready in your AI chat.

## A full example

Watch a vague worry become a sharp, buildable change.

```text
You: /opsx:explore

AI:  What would you like to explore?

You: Our checkout sometimes creates duplicate orders. I don't know why
     and I don't know how to fix it safely.

AI:  Let me dig into the checkout path...
     [reads the order service, the payment webhook handler, and the
      client submit logic]

     Here's what I found. Duplicates happen two ways:
     1. The client can submit twice (double-click, slow-network retry).
        Nothing debounces it.
     2. The payment webhook can fire more than once, and the handler
        creates an order each time. There's no idempotency key.

     Cause #2 is the dangerous one — it can charge and fulfill twice.

     Three fixes, roughly by robustness:
     - Idempotency key on order creation (handles both causes)
     - Dedup the webhook by payment-intent id (handles #2 only)
     - Client-side debounce (handles #1 only, papers over the real bug)

     Your code already stores the payment-intent id, so the idempotency
     key is a small change with the biggest payoff. Want me to scope it?

You: Yes, let's do the idempotency key.

You: /opsx:propose add-order-idempotency-key

AI:  Created openspec/changes/add-order-idempotency-key/, with a proposal
     and delta spec grounded in what we just found. Ready for implementation.
```

The starting point was "something is wrong and I am scared to touch it." Twenty seconds of exploration turned that into a named root cause, three ranked options, and a precise change. The proposal that follows is sharp because the thinking happened first.

## Tips for a good exploration

- **Bring the problem, not the solution.** "Logins feel slow" gives the AI room to investigate. "Add a Redis cache" pre-commits you to an answer you have not tested.
- **Ask for the tradeoffs out loud.** "What are the downsides of each option?" gets a more honest comparison.
- **Let it read first.** The best explorations start with the AI actually looking at your code, not guessing.
- **It is okay to bail.** If exploration reveals the idea is not worth it, that is a win — you learned it cheaply.
- **Explore again mid-change.** Stuck during `/opsx:apply`? Step back, explore a sub-problem, then return.

## The honest tradeoff

The fuzzier the task, the more explore pays off. The clearer the task, the more you can skip straight to proposing. Explore is a conversation, so it is slower than firing off `/opsx:propose` and hoping — and for work you genuinely understand, that extra step is pure overhead. Use judgment.
