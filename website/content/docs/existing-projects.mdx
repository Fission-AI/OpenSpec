**You do not document your whole codebase to start. You write specs only for what you are about to change.** That is the single most important thing about adopting OpenSpec on an existing project, and it is why OpenSpec is built brownfield-first.

A common worry: "My app is 80,000 lines old. Do I have to write specs for all of it before OpenSpec is useful?" No. You would hate that, and so would we. OpenSpec grows your specs one change at a time. Your first change documents the slice it touches, the next documents its slice, and over months your specs fill in naturally around the work you actually do.

## The thirty-second version

```bash
cd your-existing-project
openspec init          # adds openspec/ and your AI tool's commands
```

Then, in your AI chat:

```text
/opsx:explore     # optional: have the AI read the area you'll touch
/opsx:propose <a real, small change you actually need>
/opsx:apply
/opsx:archive
```

Your specs now describe exactly the part of the system that change touched, and nothing more. That is correct. You are done worrying about the other 80,000 lines.

## Why delta-first is the whole trick

OpenSpec changes are written as **deltas** — `ADDED`, `MODIFIED`, `REMOVED`. A delta describes what is changing relative to current behavior, not the entire system.

This is exactly what brownfield work needs. You are rarely building from nothing. You are adding a field, fixing a redirect, tightening a timeout. A delta lets you specify that one change precisely without first writing a 40-page spec of everything around it.

So your `openspec/specs/` directory does not start full and complete. It starts nearly empty and accumulates. The spec for `auth/` becomes thorough only after you have made several auth changes — which is exactly when you want it thorough.

## Your first change on a real codebase

Pick something small and real. Not a toy, not a rewrite. A change you were going to make this week anyway.

<Steps>

<Step>

### Let the AI read the area

This is where `/opsx:explore` earns its keep on a large or unfamiliar codebase. Point it at the part you are about to touch and let it map how things work before proposing anything.

```text
You: /opsx:explore

You: I need to add rate limiting to our public API, but I'm not sure
     how requests currently flow through the middleware.

AI:  Let me trace it... [reads the router, middleware stack, and config]
     Requests hit Express, pass through auth middleware, then your
     controllers. There's no rate-limiting layer today. The cleanest
     insertion point is a middleware right after auth. Want me to scope it?
```

The AI now understands your actual structure, so the proposal it writes fits *your* code, not a generic template.

</Step>

<Step>

### Propose, build, archive

```text
/opsx:propose add-api-rate-limiting
/opsx:apply
/opsx:archive
```

After archiving, you have a real spec for your rate-limiting behavior — born from a change you needed anyway.

</Step>

</Steps>

## Prefer a guided tour? Use onboard

If you would rather watch the whole loop happen on your own code with narration, the expanded command `/opsx:onboard` does exactly that. It scans your codebase for a small, safe improvement, then walks you through proposing, building, and archiving it, explaining each step.

```bash
openspec config profile   # select the expanded workflows
openspec update           # apply them to this project
```

Then in chat: `/opsx:onboard`. It is the gentlest possible introduction on a real project, and it leaves you with a genuine (small) change you can keep or discard.

## "But I already have requirements docs"

Maybe you have a PRD, an SRS, a formal spec, even TLA+ models. Good. You do not import them wholesale, and you do not throw them away either.

Treat existing docs as **source material for exploration**, not as specs to convert. When you start a change, point the AI at the relevant section and let it shape a focused OpenSpec delta from it. Your original documents stay where they are as background.

The honest reason: OpenSpec specs are deliberately behavior-first and scoped to changes. A 40-page PRD is a different artifact with a different job. Forcing a one-time bulk conversion tends to produce a large, stale spec nobody trusts. Letting specs grow from real changes keeps them accurate.

## Organizing specs in a big codebase

Specs live under `openspec/specs/`, grouped by **domain** — a logical area that matches how your team thinks about the system. You do not have to design the whole taxonomy up front. Create a domain folder when your first change in that area needs one.

- **By feature area:** `auth/`, `payments/`, `search/`
- **By component:** `api/`, `frontend/`, `workers/`
- **By bounded context:** `ordering/`, `fulfillment/`, `inventory/`

Pick whatever makes a newcomer nod. You can refine later.

## Monorepos and work that spans repos

For a monorepo, the simplest model is one `openspec/` directory at the repo root, with domains that map to your packages or services. That covers most teams.

If your work genuinely spans **multiple repositories**, OpenSpec has a beta [**stores**](/docs/stores) feature: planning lives in its own standalone repo that any of your code repos can reference. It is beta, so treat its commands and state as evolving.

## A few honest cautions

<Callout type="warn">
**Resist the urge to back-fill everything.** Writing specs for code you are not changing feels productive and usually is not — those specs go stale because nothing forces them to track reality. Let real changes drive your specs.
</Callout>

- **Keep early changes small.** Your first few changes are as much about learning the rhythm as shipping.
- **Commit `openspec/` to git.** Your specs and archive belong in version control alongside the code they describe.
- **Give the AI context.** On a large codebase with strong conventions, fill in `openspec/config.yaml`'s `context:` so every proposal respects your stack and patterns. See [Customization](/docs/customization).
